How It All Started

In 2014, a Penn student named Bryan Choo decided to start hosting dinners for his friends. Each friend would bring an ingredient from his or her fridge and the group would piece together a Frankenstein of a meal. Bryan held these dinners regularly and dubbed it the Dinner Project. When he graduated in May 2016, he handed the Project off to two of his younger peers, Wilson (Alex) Fisher and Daniel Fradin.

Alex and Daniel liked the idea, but changed it up a bit. Instead of only inviting friends, Alex and Daniel invited a mix of friends and acquaintences they didn't know well- students who had interesting discussion points in class or were both athletes and cooks or had an absurd knowledge of coffee. They kept the dinners intimate (partly because they lived in a teeny apartment) and established the 'no-phone' rule to encourage discussion. They turned the dinners into potlucks, and the Dinner Project as it exists today was born.

Naomi Pohl was studying abroad in New Zealand as this transition occurred. She lived in an apartment with 4 other students, 2 Americans and 2 Kiwis. Each weeknight one person would cook dinner for the entire apartment. She grew to love the tradition of eating dinner together and searched for a way to bring this experience back to Penn with her. Through Bryan, she connected with Alex and Daniel. As soon as she arrived back on campus in January 2017, the trio started hosting dinners in Naomi's (more spacious) apartment.

When Alex, Daniel and Naomi graduated from Penn in 2019, Anjali Berdia took the reigns.

What It's Like Today

Today, dinners are held bi-monthly. We have held 20 dinners and have hosted over 100 students. At the end of every dinner, we pass around a notebook. In this notebook, each attendee writes the names of 2 or 3 students who they think would enjoy the Dinner Project. We invite a subset of these recommendations to the next dinner, which helps the Dinner Project grow organically. We adhere to our mission statement:



We strive to create a place, a haven of sorts, a community even, of interesting and interested, not necessarily like-minded, but open-minded people who are willing to take a break from their fast-paced, high-pressured, and extremely competitive environment, come together over a meal, preferably home-made, throw small-talk out the window, and learn about each otherâ€™s passions. We call this The Dinner Project. Leave your phones at the door."
